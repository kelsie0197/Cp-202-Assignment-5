Derivatives and Anti Derivatives

Derivatives - gives the rate of change or slope of a function at a given point. Can be used to find slope of a line.
Basic Rules: 1. Constant Rule: The derivative of a constant is zero; that is, for a constant c
2. Constant Multiple Rule: The derivative of a constant multiplied by a function is the constant multiplied by the derivative of the original function
3. Multiple Rule: The derivative of f(ax) is given by a times the derivative of f(x)
4. Sum/Difference Rules: The derivative of the sum of two functions is the sum of the derivatives of the two functions. Likewise, the derivative of the difference of two functions is the difference of the derivatives of the two functions.
5. Product Rule: The derivative of the product of two functions is NOT the product of the functions' derivatives; rather, it is g(x)f'(x)+ g'(x)f(x)
6. Quotient Rule: The derivative of the quotient of two functions is NOT the quotient of the functions' derivatives; rather, it is [g(x)f'(x)+ g'(x)f(x)]/ g'(x)^2
7. Chain Rule: The chain rule is used to differentiate composite functions. As such, it is a vital tool for differentiating most functions of a certain complexity. It is the derivative of the outside function times the derivative of the inside function.

Anti Derivatives - taking a derivative back to its original function. With antiderivatives it is important to add a plus c to the equation since any constants would have been gone due to derivation.
Basic Rules: If the antiderivative of f(x) is F(x), and the antiderivative of g(x) is G(x) then
The antiderivative of af(x) + bg(x) is aF(x) + bG(x) (for any a, b)
The antiderivative of f(ax + b) is 1 a F(ax + b) As a result:
• If f(x) = (dx + b) a then F(x) = 1 d (dx+b) a+1 a+1 + C (unless a = −1)
• If f(x) = 1 ax+b then F(x) = 1 a ln(ax + b) + C
• If f(x) = e ax+b then F(x) = 1 a e ax+b + C
• If f(x) = cos(ax + b) then F(x) = 1 a sin(ax + b) + C
• If f(x) = sin(ax + b) then F(x) = − 1 a cos(ax + b) + C
• If f(x) = sec2 (ax + b) then F(x) = 1 a tan(ax + b) + C

Existence of Limits

Limits are the y value a function approaches at a given x value. Limits do not always exist:

This could be due to the one-sided limits are not equal
The function "jumps" at the point
The function doesn't approach a finite value
Another common situation when limits do not exist involves the function "blowing up" to infinity or - infinity, 
then the graph is characterized by a vertical asymptote at x= c.
The function doesn't approach a particular value (oscillation),
the general picture is that function takes on multiple values which are far away from each other, even when its argument moves closer and closer to x value.

Example:
lim(x-> 1-)=(x2+2x-3)/(x-1)
= (x-1)(x+3)/-(x-1)
=-(x+3)
=-4 (since x approaches 1)
Explanation: first solve any absolute values with side x is approaching (negative or positive side),
then factor as much as you can, then simplify and plug in x value at end.

Logarithmic Laws

What is a Logarithm?

A logarithm is an alternate way of showing a relationship between numbers involved in exponents. Let’s take on a simple example, 24 = 16 In this example we have 2 as the base, 4 as the exponent, and 16 as the power. Ask yourself, 2 to the power of what is 16? Instead of using trial and error, logarithms help answer this question accurately. Log2(16) is equivalent to the question 2 to the power of what is 16? Hence we have the following equivalent relationship:

Log2(16)=4 if and only if 24 = 16

General Definition

Loga(b)=c if and only if ac = b


Like exponents, logarithms have useful properties to help us simplify and solve logarithmic expressions and equations respectively.
